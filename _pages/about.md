My research applies network analysis to better understand how regional trade systems function and respond to disruption. In particular, I examine how border closures, disease outbreaks, droughts, and other climatic shocks influence agricultural trade across West Africa. Understanding geographic patterns in trade networks and how those patterns influence network structure and resilience is critical for ensuring food security despite environmental or economic shocks. 

I am a Teaching Assistant for the UF Geography department and I have used my expertise in agricultural geography to develop the course materials for the undergraduate course “Hungry Planet – Geography of Crops”. This course combines knowledge on plant biology with geographic theories and skills so that students can understand how the geography of crops has evolved over time, and how it may change in the future. 

I hold a Master of Sustainable Development Practice from the University of Florida. My academic background also includes a bachelor’s degree in Ecology from Columbia University. Before beginning my PhD, I worked as a project and research assistant with the USAID-funded Feed the Future Innovation Lab for Livestock Systems, where I supported research on livestock systems and development.

Outside of my research, I enjoy cooking, painting, and jogging.
